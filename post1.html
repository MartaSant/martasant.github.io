The Hidden Cost of Cheap Food: Inside the Problem of Overproduction

Cheap prices and big promotions look like a win for everyone, but behind them there is a system that often produces more food than we can eat – and someone else is paying the difference.

Overproduction • November 20, 2025

Why “good deals” aren’t always good news

Walk into any supermarket and you’ll see them everywhere:

  • “Buy 2, get 1 free”
  • “Family size – super value!”
  • €0.39 pasta, €0.99 milk, huge packs of snacks at irresistible prices

It looks like a celebration of abundance. Food is cheaper, more available, and more varied than ever before.

But there’s a side of this story we don’t see on the price tag: overproduction – making more food than we can reasonably eat – and the long chain of consequences that follow.

In this post, we’ll look behind the apparent convenience of cheap food and explore what it really costs in terms of waste, environment, workers, and consumers themselves.

The paradox of abundance

On the surface, cheap food feels like a win:

  • We can fill the trolley without emptying our wallet.
  • Supermarkets look full, clean, safe and efficient.
  • Promotions make us feel clever for “saving money”.

But behind that abundance, there is a paradox:

  • Farmers may be paid so little that their work barely covers production costs.
  • Tons of food are discarded at different stages of the chain (field, factory, distribution, home).
  • Environmental pressure increases: land use, water use, greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Consumers get used to a distorted idea of what food “should” cost.

In other words: what looks cheap at the checkout isn’t necessarily cheap for the system as a whole.

How the system creates overproduction

Food overproduction doesn’t happen by accident. It is built into the way our food system works.

Here are some of the mechanisms that push towards “always more”:

1. Competing on price

Large retailers and brands often compete primarily on price. To keep products cheap and shelves full, they need:

  • High volumes: the more they produce and sell, the more they can dilute costs.
  • Standardised products: fewer variations, more uniformity, easier large-scale production.
  • Tight margins: everyone in the chain is pressured to cut costs somewhere.

This race to the bottom encourages producing more than is actually needed, just to maintain market share and negotiating power.

2. Promotions that move volume, not necessarily value

Promotions like “3×2”, “buy one, get one free” or “family pack” are designed to:

  • Stimulate extra purchases (often of products we wouldn’t have bought otherwise).
  • Empty warehouses to make space for new stock.
  • Help brands and retailers appear “cheap” compared to competitors.

On paper, this moves volume. In reality, part of that extra volume may:

  • Never be sold in time (and be discarded by the retailer).
  • End up in the bin at home because we bought more than we could consume.

The system counts the product leaving the shelf as success, even if it later becomes waste.

3. Strict aesthetic and quality standards

To meet consumer expectations and branding strategies, many products must look “perfect”:

  • Fruits and vegetables with the right size, shape and colour.
  • Biscuits and snacks with consistent appearance.
  • Packaged foods with very precise texture and visual standards.

Anything that doesn’t fit these criteria may be:

  • Redirected to other uses (processing, animal feed, industry).
  • Sold at a lower price.
  • Or simply thrown away.

Perfection in appearance often means loss in the background.

4. Contracts and planning

Food production requires planning months or years in advance:

  • Farmers sign contracts to supply fixed quantities.
  • Factories schedule production based on forecasts.
  • Retailers order large volumes to secure better prices.

Forecasts, however, are never perfect. When demand is overestimated, the system has created more food than it can sell – and someone has to absorb that loss.

The hidden costs the shelf price doesn’t show

When we pay very little for food, it’s easy to forget that someone else may be paying the difference.

Let’s look at some of these “invisible” costs.

1. Environmental cost

Overproduction doesn’t just mean “a few extra boxes lying around”. To produce food, we use:

  • Land – fields that could be used differently, or not used at all.
  • Water – irrigation, cleaning, processing.
  • Energy – machinery, transport, refrigeration.
  • Inputs – fertilisers, pesticides, packaging materials.

When food ends up discarded, all those resources have been spent for nothing. The emissions, pollution and resource use remain, even if the product never reaches your plate.

In a world where we talk more and more about climate change and sustainability, producing more than we need is like heating a house with the windows open.

2. Social cost for workers and farmers

Low prices and high volumes often translate into pressure on:

  • Farmers, who may struggle to cover production costs, especially for commodities (wheat, milk, tomatoes, etc.).
  • Seasonal and migrant workers, who may work under harsh conditions for very low wages.
  • Small producers, who can’t compete with the scale and bargaining power of large companies.

Cheap food can hide situations where the real cost is borne by those at the beginning of the chain – the people who grow, harvest, process and pack our food.

3. Cost for consumers’ relationship with food

Overproduction and constant promotions also have subtle effects on how we relate to food:

  • We get used to the idea that “real price” is the discounted one, and anything else feels expensive.
  • We lose the perception of seasonality; if strawberries are always available, their value feels lower.
  • Food becomes more of a product and less of something connected to land, work and time.

When food is always abundant and cheap, it’s easier to waste it without thinking too much. Throwing away a product that cost very little feels less serious – even if the real cost was much higher.

4. Nutritional and quality trade-offs

To keep prices low while meeting expectations of taste, convenience and long shelf life, producers may:

  • Use cheaper ingredients.
  • Rely more on ultra-processing.
  • Reformulate products to be hyper-palatable (high in salt, sugar, fats).

This doesn’t mean all cheap food is automatically “bad” and all expensive food is “good”. Reality is more nuanced.

But overproduction in a low-price system tends to favour products that are cheap to produce and easy to sell in large quantities, not necessarily those that are best for long-term health.

It’s not just about “individual responsibility”

At this point, it might be tempting to say:

“Ok, so I just have to stop wasting food and stop buying promotions.”

Individual actions matter, but it’s important to be clear: overproduction is a structural problem, not just a matter of personal guilt.

You didn’t design pricing policies.
You didn’t decide supermarket standards or marketing strategies.
You didn’t create the rules of international trade.

Blaming individual consumers alone is like blaming passengers for the route chosen by the bus driver.

What we can do, however, is understand the system better and gradually adjust our choices – while also supporting broader changes.

What can be done? System and everyday actions

Solving overproduction and its hidden costs requires action on many levels. Here are some directions – not as magic solutions, but as possible steps.

On a systemic level (policies, companies, institutions)

Support for fair prices
Mechanisms that allow farmers to cover production costs and invest in better practices – without forcing them into a constant volume race.

Incentives to reduce waste along the chain
Better planning, redistribution of surplus, and transparency about where and how much waste happens.

Rethinking promotions
Moving from volume-based promotions (buy more!) to value-based approaches (better quality, fairer origin, less waste).

Clearer information
Labels and communication that don’t just sell a story, but explain more honestly what we are paying for.

As individual consumers we don’t control these levers directly, but we can support companies and initiatives that move in this direction and bring these topics into public conversation.

In everyday life (our sphere of influence)

We don’t have to change everything overnight. But we can:

  • Buy with more intention – Ask: “Will I really eat this before it expires?” before falling for “extra-large” packs just because they are cheap.
  • Plan a little – Even a simple shopping list can reduce impulse buying driven by promotions.
  • Normalise “less perfect” food – Choose “imperfect” fruit and vegetables when possible – they taste the same and help reduce waste.
  • Value what we already have at home – Cook with what’s in the cupboard and fridge before buying more. Leftover-based meals are a quiet way to resist overproduction.
  • Support better practices when we can – Sometimes it means choosing a product that costs slightly more but comes from a more transparent or fair chain.

These are not heroic actions. They are small shifts in perspective that, repeated over time and across many people, send a signal to the market.

Cheap for whom?

The next time you see a shockingly low price or an aggressive promotion, you don’t need to feel guilty for being tempted. It’s normal – the system is designed to work that way.

But you can pause for a second and ask:

  • “Cheap for whom?”
  • “Who is paying the difference?”

Sometimes the answer is: the environment, workers, farmers, or even ourselves in the form of waste and poorer food relationships.

This post isn’t about telling you what to buy. It’s about pulling back the curtain on a mechanism that often stays invisible behind the price tag.
